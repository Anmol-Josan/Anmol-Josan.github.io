Exhibit Overview

The Singh Twins Give Voice to the British Sikh Diaspora

Born in 1966 to Sikh immigrant parents in England, Amrit and Rabindra Kaur Singh, known collectively as The Singh Twins, faced discrimination as the only non-Catholic students in their school. When they pursued art education, their professors dismissed their work as "too traditional" and "unacceptable," insisting they abandon Indian miniature painting for Western contemporary styles.

They refused to assimilate. Instead, The Singh Twins created what they call "Past-Modern" art, work that honors centuries-old techniques while addressing contemporary issues of identity, colonialism, and marginalization. Their intricate paintings preserve cultural heritage while giving voice to communities that dominant narratives have tried to silence.

"We use the visual language of our ancestors to tell stories that official histories refuse to acknowledge."

Like Zora Neale Hurston, who preserved Black Southern dialect as literature rather than mockery, The Singh Twins insist that their cultural traditions deserve respect in contemporary art spaces. Both artists demand that audiences meet them where they are, refusing to translate their culture into the dominant language. When you force a culture to speak in someone else's voice, you lose something essential.

This exhibition explores six key works that demonstrate how The Singh Twins transform silence into sound, making visible the lives, struggles, and contributions of the British Sikh diaspora. Each piece resists erasure by placing marginalized figures at the center of historical narratives, painted with the same elaborate detail once reserved for emperors and royalty.

Artifact Gallery

Six Works of Resistance

2018 Digital fabric lightbox artwork Slaves of Fashion Series

Cotton: Threads of Change

This life-sized portrait features Princess Sophia Duleep Singh, a British-born Sikh suffragette and daughter of the last Maharaja of Punjab. She stands draped in cotton textiles, surrounded by symbolic imagery connecting the cotton trade to colonialism, slavery, and modern exploitation. Behind her, cotton fields represent both historical enslavement and contemporary forced labor.

What It Represents

The silenced story of a Sikh princess who fought for women's rights while her own community's history was being erased by British colonialism. Princess Sophia's story represents the intersection of Sikh, British, and feminist identity, a voice that was silenced by colonial history until The Singh Twins brought her forward.

How It Resists Silence

By placing a forgotten Sikh woman at the center of suffragette history, The Singh Twins challenge the white-centered narrative of feminism and make visible the contributions of South Asian women to social justice movements. The elaborate detail demands that viewers recognize her dignity and importance.

2018 Digital fabric lightbox artwork Slaves of Fashion Series

Indigo: The Colour of India

This striking piece depicts Mughal Queen Mumtaz Mahal wearing modern blue jeans. The detailed portrait traces the history of indigo dye, once known as "blue gold" and used as currency to buy enslaved people. The artwork reveals how denim, considered quintessentially Western, actually originated in India, where the coarse fabric was first produced in the village of Dongri (which gave us "dungaree").

What It Represents

The erasure of Indian cultural and commercial contributions to global fashion, and the silencing of workers who were exploited for their craft. Intricate patterns and symbols throughout the piece tell the story of how Indian craftsmanship was appropriated and its origins forgotten during colonialism.

How It Resists Silence

By visually connecting modern clothing to its Indian origins, The Singh Twins reclaim cultural ownership and make visible the hidden labor and creativity of South Asian people that was appropriated by Western colonial powers. The juxtaposition of historical figure and modern garment forces viewers to confront their assumptions about cultural origins.

2020 Mixed media on paper Slaves of Fashion Series

Get Your Knee Off Our Necks

Created in response to the murder of George Floyd, this satirical portrait shows Donald Trump with his knee on the neck of the Statue of Liberty, who is gagged and bound. Trump's torso is tattooed with narcissus flowers, symbolizing narcissism. The background is filled with symbols representing America's foundation on slavery and the cotton trade.

What It Represents

The continuation of colonial violence and racism in modern politics, and the silencing of marginalized communities who protest injustice. The piece draws explicit parallels between historical colonialism and contemporary racism, connecting the exploitation of South Asian laborers to the oppression of Black Americans.

How It Resists Silence

By using the visual language of political satire and historical symbolism, The Singh Twins make contemporary viewers confront uncomfortable truths about ongoing systems of oppression. They give voice to victims of state violence by connecting their struggles to broader patterns of historical exploitation.

2006 Hand-painted miniature Commission for Museum of London

EnTWINed

This complex painting reimagines a historical scene of immigrants arriving in Britain, but from an Indian perspective. Based on a 19th-century painting, The Singh Twins' version includes figures from the Indian diaspora throughout British history: Mahatma Gandhi, Indian soldiers who fought in World Wars I and II, and the artists' own grandmother and father. The skyline combines buildings from London, Birmingham, Liverpool, and Bradford, all cities with significant South Asian populations.

What It Represents

The invisible contributions of South Asian immigrants to British society and the erasure of their stories from mainstream British history. By including their own family members alongside historical figures, The Singh Twins assert that ordinary people's stories matter as much as those of famous leaders.

How It Resists Silence

By inserting South Asian figures into the visual narrative of British history, The Singh Twins challenge the dominant story that Britain was built solely by white Europeans. They make visible the multicultural reality that official histories often ignore, demanding that immigrant contributions be recognized and celebrated.

2000 Hand-painted miniature The Iqbalnama Series

Casualty of War: A Portrait of Maharaja Duleep Singh

This portrait depicts Maharaja Duleep Singh, the last ruler of the Sikh Empire in Punjab, who was forced to give up his throne as a child and spent his life in exile in Britain. The painting shows him in elaborate traditional dress, surrounded by symbols of his lost kingdom and his complicated relationship with the British Crown. The intricate details typical of Indian miniature painting contrast with the tragedy of his displacement and cultural erasure.

What It Represents

The violence of colonialism against Sikh sovereignty and the silencing of Sikh royal history and cultural identity. Duleep Singh's story represents the personal cost of empire, a child king stripped of his heritage and forced to live as a curiosity in the court of his conquerors.

How It Resists Silence

By painting Duleep Singh with the dignity and detail reserved for European royalty, The Singh Twins restore honor to a figure whose story was deliberately obscured by British colonial narratives. They preserve Sikh heritage through traditional artistic methods, insisting that their cultural traditions deserve the same respect as Western art forms.

2022 Audio interview excerpt BBC World Service

In Their Own Words: The Singh Twins Speak

In this interview, The Singh Twins discuss why they insist on being known collectively rather than as individuals, despite pressure from art professors to develop separate styles. They explain how their collaborative identity challenges Western individualism and honors Sikh values of community. They also discuss facing discrimination as the only Sikh students in their Catholic school and being told their focus on Indian miniature painting was "backward" and represented "all the taboos of contemporary Western art."

What It Represents

The silencing and marginalization of non-Western artistic traditions in European art education, and the pressure on immigrant artists to assimilate. Their story represents the experiences of countless artists who have been told their cultural heritage has no place in contemporary spaces.

How It Resists Silence

By sharing their personal story of discrimination, The Singh Twins give voice to experiences that are often kept private out of shame or fear. Their success proves that preserving heritage and challenging Western art norms can coexist, and that cultural traditions are sources of strength rather than obstacles to overcome.

Curatorial Reflection

Why These Voices Matter

When communities are silenced, we lose more than individual stories. We lose entire ways of seeing, thinking, and making meaning. The Singh Twins' work, like Zora Neale Hurston's before them, proves that giving voice to marginalized communities isn't charity or activism alone. It's essential to understanding who we are as a society.

Without Hurston's work, we would have lost the metaphors Black Southerners used, the stories they told, the way they made sense of their world through oral tradition. Without The Singh Twins, we wouldn't know about Princess Sophia Duleep Singh's role in the suffragette movement, or the Indian origins of denim, or the resistance of Sikh artists in British schools. These aren't footnotes to history. They are history itself, deliberately obscured by dominant narratives.

Preserving Culture

Both artists refuse to translate their cultures into the dominant language. Hurston preserved Black Southern dialect as literature. The Singh Twins preserved Indian miniature painting as contemporary art. Translation is erasure.

Challenging Narratives

Both artists prove that the "official" story is incomplete. Hurston showed the complex internal lives of people white literature portrayed as simple. The Singh Twins reveal the violence and appropriation that colonial histories try to hide.

Making Individuals Visible

Both artists reject stereotypes by showing the complexity of daily life. Hurston gave us Janie on her porch, Tea Cake in the fields. The Singh Twins included their own grandmother arriving in Britain alongside Mahatma Gandhi.

Every time a white author mocked Black dialect as "ignorant," it told Black Southerners their way of speaking was wrong. Every time an art professor dismissed Indian miniature painting as "backward," it told South Asian students their heritage was worthless. Both Hurston and The Singh Twins refuse that silencing. They say: our cultures are valuable, our histories are important, our voices deserve to be heard on our own terms, in our own language.

The work of giving voice to silenced communities is never finished. New generations need new artists to tell their stories, to insist on their humanity, to preserve their culture. But Hurston and The Singh Twins show us the path forward: by refusing to assimilate, by honoring tradition while addressing the present, and by creating art so powerful that the dominant culture can no longer ignore the voices it tried to silence.
